# Context Statement

I have been involved in university lecturing since 2014 and took on
course convening responsibilities in 2018 at the University of Oslo. I
(re-)joined the ANU in 2019 where I lecture large under/post-grad
courses (150-400 students) in computer systems and creative computing
and manage teams of 6-20 tutors. I have an interdisciplinary background
in computing and music which informs my research and teaching practices.

In 2020, I led efforts to pivot our computer science education to a
remote mode and documented a system of best practices for running labs
and lectures online which has been widely applied in my college and at
the ANU. My approach to remote learning is student-centred and
inclusive, providing a mix of asynchronous and synchronous content and
activities. I am particularly interested in engaging computing students
in creative and personalised projects at all stages of their higher
education and incorporating aspects of studio-based learning to computer
science.

I recently re-designed our second-year course in computer systems to
focus on creative and open-ended problem solving and established the
first sound and music course within the School of Computing. I have led
efforts to establish a revised human-centred and creative computing
pathway for all ANU computing students. This has involved developing
courses in Art and Interaction Computing and Computer Music. In this
field, I have documented the evolution of our curriculum through
publications in the Australasian Computer Music Conference. I am the
primary supervisor to four PhD students and have been supervising
Master/honours students since 2016.

# Philosophy of Teaching and Learning

I love teaching and I am passionate about finding effective and engaging
ways to teach computing. I believe that computing should be a
wide-interest subject with students with different backgrounds,
interests, and career paths. I am committed to inclusion regardless of
background and I teach at scales from 1-to-1 to 1-to-N. I use my
expertise in computing to create and explore new learning technologies.
I aim to work efficiently and strategically as a lecturer to deliver
excellence in research-led and student-centred teaching.

Computing is a creative activity, and I am passionate about framing
computing education around "making as learning", where knowledge and
skills are acquired through significant project work. This aligns well
with authentic assessment practices where computing students develop
open-ended software projects. I support these assessments with
scaffolding and explicit teaching to build confidence and also provide
scope for inquiry so that students can discover unique approaches and
implementations.

Practical computer lab sessions are the most important learning
activities in our computing programs. When developing lab activities and
mentoring tutors, I reflect on my experiences as a music student, where
I experienced a studio-based education. In computing labs I aim for
students and their tutors to build community, develop a shared
experience, communicate feedback, all while pursuing technical skills.
My goal is for students to have a strong support network and lots of
opportunities for peer learning when preparing for and completing
assessments.

Inclusion is an important part of my teaching philosophy, and I am to
create learning environments that are inclusive to students of different
backgrounds, with different levels of experience and different
interests. Computing is a core skill for interacting with the modern
world and needs to be accessible and inclusive to everybody. I take
proactive steps to adopt inclusive language and computing terminology
and to call out offensive, insensitive and discriminatory practices in
computing.

When teaching hybrid and multi-modal classes I have a student-centred
approach emphasising human interactions, combinations of asynchronous
and synchronous components, accessible remote assessment and new
resources for peer learning and collaboration between remote students. I
want to preserve a strong sense of "going on a journey together", even
when students are distributed around the world. With these approaches I
aim to support the immediate needs of students studying remotely but
also to make university more accessible to on-campus students who work,
are carers, or are disabled.

# Reflective Narrative 1 (A1, A2, A3, A4)

## Designing Programs and Courses

My work in teaching creative computing includes designing and delivering
the courses Sound and Music Computing (SMC) and Art and Interaction
Computing (AIC) as well as supervising research students. Creative
computing is relatively unexplored within computing curricula published
by our professional bodies (e.g., ACM & IEEE, 2013) and it is
challenging to provide learning material to support diverse learners
with all aspects of the subject matter (K1, K6). In 2022, I was invited
to document AIC as a case-study in transdisciplinary problem solving by
the ANU's working groups on proposed graduate attributes. I felt that
this process exposed the challenges I was facing in terms of integrating
different fields of knowledge for the students. That year, I was able to
more clearly articulate to my tutor team how the learning activities
address transdisciplinary problems, encouraging them to acknowledge the
different approaches to learning that this entails (K3, V4).

The success of these classes has encouraged me and two of my colleagues
to collaborate on creating suite of sub-programs in Human-Centred and
Creative Computing (HCCC) at the ANU which are available from 2023. This
work involved defining new expectations for what a computing graduate
might look like, including the expectation that they have knowledge and
skills in fields outside of computing (V1, K1, K2). While doing so, I
was conscious of how our programs would fit into the Australian
Qualifications Framework, particularly where we co-teach undergraduate
and master degree cohorts (K6). In our HCCC program and other courses, I
have supported my colleagues to create ways to differentiate programs
and courses so that they are appropriately designed for different levels
in alignment with the AQF and provide appropriate expectations for
students (K6). While students have yet to complete these programs, the
planning process has fed back into course planning where I have adjusted
course designs (for instance, pre-requisites and sequencing in SMC) to
include a wider variety of learners (V2). This effort resulted in a
strong interdisciplinary community emerging in my 2022 SMC class, as
noted by the students, that supported their experience through peer
learning (K2, K3).

In 2022, I also redesigned the learning activities for Computer
Organisation and Program Execution (COPE), a core undergraduate
computing course with large enrolment (\~400 students), and large
teaching team (19 tutors). Students had reported difficulties in 2019
and 2020, e.g., "I attended all lectures but... don't know how to do
labs and assignments" (2019), so I implemented an action research plan
on the learning activities and assessments (K5). As part of this plan, I
developed a clearer structure for self-paced computer lab activities
that would be delivered by my tutors. I felt that by making clear to
students which activities directly scaffolded the assessments, I could
be more explicit about the skills and knowledge they needed to develop
(K3, V3). The new structure allowed students to have their work
automatically checked off in our code management system, I feel that
this allowed my teaching team more time to assist students during their
labs (K4).

## Teaching

As a lecturer in computing at ANU, I have focussed on creating engaging
presentations for learners who have been mostly online over the last
three years. My goal in lectures is to take students on a journey,
through a topic, concept, or a computer program (K1). In early 2020, I
led efforts to switch to online lecturing, designing ways for students
to interact with me live during a streamed lecture and providing streams
on multiple platforms to suit different students (K4, V2). From student
feedback, I learned that many enjoyed streamed lectures ("lectures are
more interesting that any TV show") and engaged in text chat with me and
other students (K3). While streaming with chat did not provide real-time
conversation (compared with video conferencing), I felt that it provided
an inclusive experience for students who were more likely to chat to me
than turn their camera on. I documented my evolving approach over the
semester (Martin, 2020a), and my work was featured in my College's
newsletter and adopted by other staff.

During my remote teaching experience, I felt that live programming
during lectures, rather than only using static slides, was particularly
appropriate for this modality. During remote lectures, I construct a
program live during the lecture with students following along and making
suggestions. I think this approach supports students by explicitly
demonstrating expected code practices (State of Victoria Department of
Education and Training, 2020) and develops rapport with students who
influence the direction of coding examples and can correct the
lecturer's mistakes (Heffernan et al., 2009) (V3). In a streamed
lecture, I felt that students had the advantage of reviewing live-code
sections multiple times and of having a clearer view than in a lecture
theatre (K3). After student feedback calling my lectures "lively",
"interesting", and "engaging", I currently have a goal of spending 50%
of my lectures programming, in dialogue with students and discussing the
concepts involved (K5, V3).

My teaching in Sound and Music Computing (SMC) evolved from the
studio-based learning model I experienced as a music student and
initially I primarily focussed on students' individual projects. After
the first semester, the outcomes weren't meeting my expectations so I
changed my teaching method to encourage collaboration and peer-learning
across the semester on many low-stakes outputs (V1, K2). My teaching
changed from mainly guiding individuals, to helping groups help each
other (K3). I felt students then reached a higher standard as they could
draw from a range of ideas and could support each other in the
assessment tasks.

## Assessment and Feedback

My large computing course, COPE, has traditionally involved challenging
assignments and an in-person exam. When pivoting to online delivery, I
was encouraged by research suggesting that multiple choice questions
could assess higher-order thinking in computing (Woodford & Bancroft,
2005) and created exams that combined randomised
multiple-choice-multiple-answer (MCMA) questions with short coding and
essay questions (V3). These exams were effective assessments in terms of
differentiating learners' achievements and were highly accessible to
students as they were delivered through our LMS (K2, V2). I did feel
that students' expectation that multiple choice questions ought to be
easy led to negative feedback ("harsh", "bad", "terrible") and that some
students were confused by MCMA format questions.

In 2022, I revised the assessments in COPE to focus on divergent tasks
and supported my tutors with a detailed rubric and training in providing
good feedback for students (V1). The assessments were designed to be
authentic in that students had a genuine opportunity to design a
complete program while pursuing specific learning outcomes (K1, V3). I
felt these measures improved the quality of feedback overall and made
the meaning of marks clearer to students (K3). I implemented a
moderation process for each assessment to develop marking standards with
my tutors and to help inform how we gave feedback. I felt the formal
moderation and mentoring process with my teaching team allowed us to
maintain clearer standards (K6). This process was aided by our code
management system GitLab, and Lucy, in-house software (I lead
development) to facilitate code-based assessments. In 2021, I documented
and presented Lucy to my colleagues through school meetings, and it is
now used in other large undergraduate courses within our school (K4). On
reflection, I felt the assignments in COPE were more successful in terms
of providing good feedback and students reported that they "enjoyed the
freedom" of the "challenging but achievable" tasks. In my interaction
with colleagues I have advocated that more progressive assessment
practices within computing, supported by good learning technologies, can
lead to both better students outcomes and lower stress for teachers
(V1).

I noticed in 2022 while teaching COPE that extension requests are
significant task for course convenors to handle. Examining the extent of
this problem, I counted 122 extension requests over that semester, with
many including too little or too much information (K5). Requests were
often repeated and I felt that this was causing unnecessary stress for
me and my students. In response, I developed clear guidelines for
extensions, and advocated that the School of Computing should adopt an
extension request application developed at another ANU school (K2, V3).
I supported professional staff in our school to adapt the application
for our needs. This application was adopted and I led efforts to bring
other academics on board through a school meeting presentation
advocating for clearer extension guidelines. I felt that this process
allowed tutors to handle most extension requests in a generous way that
supported the students' learning (K3) without negative impact on
assessment processes.

## Learning Environments

Self-paced computer lab classes are a core aspect of computing courses
at ANU. The COVID-19 pandemic presented a real challenge to this mode of
learning that normally involves regular check-ins between tutors and
students, impromptu mini-lectures from tutors, and the ability to
monitor student progress during a lab (K2). I led my team of 19 tutors
to develop a new approach to online lab teaching in 2020 that was
adopted as best-practice within our school and by academics around the
ANU (K4). Our online labs provided a virtual learning environment where
students were able to connect with their tutors and each other while
completing self-paced learning activities. I sought feedback from tutors
and students in our early labs and found issues with students not
engaging and ending up feeling abandoned or left behind (K5, V3). In
response, I mentored my staff in intentional practices to check-in with
every student regularly and record an introduction for each lab that
could be replayed by students who arrive late (V1). Following these
experiences, my tutor team and I received an award (ANU College of
Engineering and Computer Science Remote Teaching and Student Experience
Awards, Dean's Award 2020) for our support for students during remote
teaching.

In SMC, a recent example shows how collaboration environments such as
Microsoft Teams, can also be applicable in an in-person workshop
environment (K4). In early iterations of SMC, we (the co-convenors) felt
that our students had not developed collaborative skills well over the
semester leading to difficulties in the final project for this course.
We were inspired by research reporting a "Beat Cypher" songwriting class
structure (Murphy, 2019) to create a more intentionally collaborative
environment in our creative computing class (K2). The following year, we
redesigned our workshops so that students needed to collaborate with new
people each week so that they would get to know everybody in their class
and gain more experience in communicating their own ideas by the end of
the semester (K3). Since then, the collaborative nature of the class has
evolved. In 2022, with more students in the class, I assigned students
to a small "ensemble" to work with throughout the semester while also
collaborating with others during workshops. I gave each ensemble an area
within the class's Microsoft Team to upload code, files and media as a
default way to share work. I supported this technical affordance by
encouraging the students within workshops to use the Team whenever they
needed to share their work or ask questions (K4). This fully open
collaborative approach led to opportunities for peer-learning and
inspiration throughout the semester (V2). The final assessments for SMC
were stronger in terms of the amount of interaction between group
members and the engagement of the whole class. In student feedback one
class member noted that "the sense of community and the culture of
sharing and assisting each other is unlike any other course I've taken."
(K5)

My work in teaching Sound and Music Computing has evolved through a

Interfaces for Musical Expression---web officer) community and [ACMA

several NIME conferences and integrated aspects of workshops and

a workshop on "Making Predictive NIMEs with Neural Networks",
transforming my research work in creative application of generative AI
into teaching materials and working through them with other NIME
researchers (Martin, 2019) (K1, V3). This experience provided feedback
on my teaching materials from other experienced teachers, as well as the
challenge of refining work for a demanding audience and I was able to
use these materials with subsequent individual project and research
students. Later in 2019, I published research at the ACMA conference
related to a multi-disciplinary approach to computer music education at
ANU (Swift et al., 2019). This work articulated the goals of our
computer music course at ANU and set the tone for our future development
in sound and music computing education (K2, V4, see Case Study 2).

In my other computer science courses COPE (Computer Organisation and
Program Execution) and AIC (Art and Interaction Computing), I have
engaged in professional development at ANU and evaluation of my
practices. This include the Advancing Learning and Teaching program that
I completed in 2021 and Leading Learning and Teaching in 2022. Both of
these experiences incorporated a wide range of research and scholarship
in pedagogy (K3), and higher education policy (K4). I found it
particularly useful to engage with educators outside of my discipline to
develop a wider perspective on higher education. For COPE, I applied my
professional development in an action research plan that I implemented
in 2022 (K6), updating the course design to take advantage of research
in assessment and high-impact teaching (K2, V3, see Case Study 1). I
relayed my plans and findings through external talks (Martin, 2022) and
discussions within the ANU computing faculty.

For AIC, a first-year elective course in creating interactive art with
computers open to all students at ANU, I have been focussed in
incorporating research-led elements from creative arts, interaction
design, and human-computer interaction into my teaching (V3). While many
of these concepts have been familiar to me as a creative practitioner, I
realised that within a computing school, I needed to scaffold
motivations and rationale for creating interactive computer artworks to
support students in addition to teaching technical skills in coding
(K2). One outcome has been a bibliography in Art and Interaction
Computing, that I use as a reference list throughout the course (Martin,
2021) which could form the basis of a future publication (K1).

# Case study 1: A creative hybrid take on computer systems education

This case study is about integrating creative and open-ended assessment
tasks into a difficult and theory-heavy part of the standard computer
science curriculum while managing remote teaching to give students at
all levels better opportunity to express themselves in their learning.
In short, this is about leaning into creative assessment tasks and
making-as-learning to improve student learning through explicit
teaching, scaffolding and activating students' imaginations.

Computer Organisation and Program Execution (COPE) is a core part of the
computing "Body of Knowledge" (ACM & IEEE, 2013). At ANU, all of our
second-year undergraduate computing students (300-400 students) take
this course so there is a high degree of diversity among learners and
wide variation in their level of preparation. The ANU's COPE course has
some notable features including a focus on using physical hardware (a
microcontroller board) rather than a simulator for experimenting with
low-level computer programming. For high-performing students, COPE can
be "the best course I've done at ANU", but it's also a difficult course
with some students finding the material "too hard" or "too heavy". I
taught this course in 2019, 2020, and 2022. In the latter two editions,
I put interventions in place to cope with the influence of the COVID-19
pandemic and in response to poor outcomes including students failing or
cheating.

In 2020, the focus was remote teaching. The ban on international
students from China directly entering Australia came into effect just
before the start of semester and affected all computing courses very
seriously. I led a group of tutors before the semester to plan remote
computer labs for students studying outside of Australia. Our goal was
to enable responsive support from tutors for students who were
undertaking self-paced computer lab tasks. We developed a protocol for
tutors to initiate video calls and screen sharing to provide support for
individual students while keeping a call open with other tutors for load
balancing our large lab classes. The methods we developed in these early
weeks of the semester were adopted as best-practice in the School of
Computing and others around the university (Martin, 2020b), and were
still in use for hybrid teaching in 2022. Later in 2020, when the
university was closed for teaching, we were able to pivot to a
completely online mode while retaining individual attention to students.
One takeaway from our remote lab method was identifying that although
technology like Teams might *enable* video and text chat, the teacher
must intentionally initiate interactions that may have occurred
naturally in a classroom by looking over student's shoulder at their
work.

Throughout 2020 and 2022, students told me how much they enjoyed my
online lecture streams (e.g., "Charles was an amazing lecturer").
Reflecting on why this is the case, I had put a lot of effort into
technical aspects of my lectures in terms of content and presentation,
but students seem to particularly respond to dynamism and rapport in my
lectures as much as the specific examples and concepts I discuss. These
two aspects of lecturing are known to support effective teaching
(Heffernan et al., 2009). In the context of online lecturing to a large
class I feel that "rapport" could be about how much students feel
connected to me, speaking to them over YouTube and never meeting in
person.

While we survived 2020, I noticed that a cohort of students were falling
behind and failing COPE multiple times. For the 2022 course, a change in
the hardware microcontroller to the visually engaging BBC MicroBit
(Martin, 2022) provided an opportunity to revise the assessments and
learning activities to help struggling students meet the learning
outcomes. Computing courses often focus on inquiry-based learning ("I
give you the tools, you figure out how to use them") and I felt that
this tends to work better with advanced students. I wanted to focus on
explicit teaching and scaffolding the assessment tasks more clearly with
labs---both "high impact" teaching strategies (State of Victoria
Department of Education and Training, 2020)---so that all students would
be prepared and confident in the coding assignments. I restructured the
assignments to focus *only* on coding a creative program with the
MicroBit: first a "sound and light show" on the simple display and then
an interactive "digital pet". Previous assignments for COPE had included
creative tasks as an extension task, with most students focussing on
other convergent tasks with the microcontrollers that also exposed them
to the temptation for plagiarism. In my new design, similar convergent
tasks were transferred to the labs and I supported my tutors to
encourage students to collaborate and share knowledge and focus more
intentionally on the skills needed to begin the assignment.

These assessment tasks aimed to include more divergent creative thinking
across the cohort by adopting elements of STEAM education (Harris &
Bruin, 2017). These tasks gave all students the confidence to meet the
learning requirements by applying skills they had practiced in labs
while allowing inquisitive learners the chance to extend their knowledge
significantly and come up with quite amazing submissions. The results
were better than previous years with many fewer academic integrity
issues.

Computing education and particularly our core subjects focussed on
technical knowledge often feature convergent assessment tasks based on
technical challenges. In COPE, this approach had led to lower levels of
achievement and plagiarism among our large class. My work implementing
engaging remote teaching and creative assessment tasks led to better
outcomes with students even throughout the uncertain years of COVID-19
making this course "surprisingly interesting given the topic". I have
engaged in disseminating this approach to computer systems teaching at
the ANU (Martin, 2020b) and at the University of Oslo (Martin, 2022).

# Case study 2: Rebooting computer music education

Sound and Music Computing is my research and artistic field and although
I have seen interest from students in this topic through workshops and
projects the ANU has not traditionally had a course in this area. In
contrast with other creative computing areas such as graphics, sound and
music computing is not part of a typical computing curriculum (ACM &
IEEE, 2021). This case study discusses my journey towards creating a
Sound and Music Computing course within the ANU School of Computing
between 2019 and 2023.

When I returned to the ANU in 2019, I saw that there was potential to
combine efforts from academics in computing (myself and Ben Swift) and
the ANU School of Music (Alec Hunter). I had previously collaborated
with Alec Hunter to create co-curricular music computing workshops and
projects in 2014-2015 and Ben Swift had created an "ANU Laptop Ensemble"
under the School of Computing's "TechLauncher" project course. I
introduced the goal to consolidate these efforts and provide a course
where computing or music students could study together in a for-credit
computer music from a computer music perspective. To achieve this, we
co-opted undergraduate project course codes from the Schools of
Computing and Music that could be used for a co-taught class with a
small group (6) of music and computing students.

In contrast with our previous projects and other laptop ensemble courses
(Trueman, 2007) involving set repertoire or pure improvisation, our
laptop ensemble involved collaborative performance of individual
computer music creations. I felt that music students would benefit from
a focus on tool-creation, often absent from music technology curricula
focussed on tool-use. For computing students, the course emphasised
reflective skills in a STEAM (Harris & Bruin, 2017) context; I felt that
students would benefit from closely connecting software design with
human interaction. The story and motivations up to this point was
published as "Two Perspectives on Rebooting Computer Music Education"
(Swift et al., 2019) at the Australasian Computer Music Conference
(ACMC). In this paper, we discussed approaches to "laptop ensemble"
classes at universities around the world, the different roles and
perspectives that students can take in these classes, and justified our
approach in terms of benefits to music and computing students.

In the 2019 edition the students were successful in creating
performances for their assessment but we felt they hadn't reached their
potential in terms of collaboration or in exploring sophisticated
computer music concepts. At that year's ACMC, an academic from Box Hill
Institute introduced us to "Beat Cyphers", a hip-hop inspired rapid
iteration structure for teaching song writing where students are asked
to create a song each week of the semester following creative prompts
(Murphy, 2019). In 2020, we adapted the Beat Cypher structure, setting
students 1-minute weekly computer music assignments with prompts that
follow major topics in sound and music computing. Each week, the
students would listen to everybody's submission, engage in a
"crit"-style discussion, and then use each other's code from the
submissions to create new group performances in class. We aimed to
change the "ensemble performance" aspect of the class from a scary
summative assessment into the main learning activity and weekly
formative assessment. This structure emphasised from week 1 that
students needed to *make* computer music to learn, and to perform and
listen to refine their understanding.

The 2020 and 2021 versions of this course shared the workshop style
delivery and were marked by the development of shared understanding and
identity by the students over the semester as *The ANU Laptop Ensemble*.
These courses had small numbers of students (9 and 10 respectively) by
computer science standards and so the students had a high degree of
autonomy in terms of the shape of their final performance and how met
the brief of creating "ensemble" computer music pieces. The 2020 edition
faced challenges of emergency remote delivery where students took on a
lot of responsibility for self-organising distributed recordings of
their final performances while the 2021 cohort performed their final
concert in a local bar and music venue. While the flexible and unusual
aspects of the course were positive for the small cohort, I felt that
larger numbers of students would challenge this design.

In 2022, plans were finalised to move the course from the shared special
topics code to its own course code which would attract more students
from our new Human Centred and Creative Computing curriculum. The 2022
edition attracted 22 students, more than doubling the previous year, and
making some of the course features, such as the single final concert,
impractical. While the workshop structure was retained, I assigned
students to ensembles of 4 or 5 performers early in the course and
created five separate final concerts. I recruited two tutors to assist
in our workshops, supporting them to assist these small ensemble groups,
and asked the groups to use Microsoft Teams for storing and sharing
their videos, code, and ideas. That year, students had less flexibility
in terms of choosing the configuration of their final assessment but I
felt that there were more consistent outcomes and fairer comparisons
between their achievements. The new Sound and Music Computing course
commenced in 2023 with the same structure and 70 students.

Having developed Sound and Music Computing over five years, we have
learned that it is possible to integrate learning approaches normally
associated with creative arts or studio-based learning into computing
and we have started scaling to cope with larger student cohorts. We were
able to respond to weaknesses in the assessment outcomes not by
introducing harsher assessment standards, but by changing the
expectations of how students would engage with learning activities to
more explicitly prepare them for their summative assessments. Our
students have particularly appreciated collaboration and peer-learning
in this course noting that the "sense of community and the culture of
sharing and assisting each other is unlike any other course I've taken".
